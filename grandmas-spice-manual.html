1. Introduction

Grandma’s Spice is a clipper that refuses to throw detail away. Where a standard hard clipper flattens everything above the threshold into a featureless plateau, Grandma’s Spice folds the overshoot back into the permitted range — reflecting it off the threshold, as many times as it takes. High-frequency texture and transient detail riding on the peaks survive, even when you push the input far past the rails.

Around that core sit a Delta control that sets how much folded detail is blended back (with a dedicated EQ that shapes only the overshoot), a four-flavour saturation pad (Linear, JFET, Op-Amp, Transformer), three 8-band parametric EQs, oversampling up to 8×, and a live spectrum analyser and oscilloscope.

2. Quick start

  1. Insert Grandma’s Spice on a drum bus, synth, or full mix.
  2. Lower Threshold until the meters and spectrum show clipping activity.
  3. Raise Delta to bring folded detail back in; cut it for a harder, cleaner clip.
  4. Click Fold Mode to audition the three fold shapes (see section 5).
  5. Open the Saturation tab and drag the pad node toward JFET, Op-Amp, or Transformer for analog colour.
  6. Balance the level with Output; raise Oversampling if you hear aliasing on bright material.

3. Interface overview

The window is a fixed-size four-quadrant grid:

  • Top left — the main drive knobs (Input, Threshold, Output, Delta), Fold Mode button, oversampling selector, and stereo input/output meters.
  • Top right — the spectrum analyser, overlaying the dry and processed signals.
  • Bottom left — the tab strip: Input, Delta, and Output EQs plus the Saturation pad.
  • Bottom right — the oscilloscope.

The Scopes button hides or shows the two analyser panels; with scopes hidden the window collapses to a compact half-width layout. Hovering any control shows a tooltip explaining it.

5. The three fold modes

The fold shape decides how the overshoot reflects off the threshold:

Mode 1 — Triangle

The classic wave-folder zig-zag: the overshoot reflects with a sharp corner at the rail. The brightest and most aggressive mode — dense upper harmonics, real bite. Great on drums and anything that should cut.

Mode 2 — Sine

A smooth sinusoidal reflection — the folded peak lands exactly at the threshold with no hard corner. Rounder, warmer harmonics that stay musical as you keep pushing the input.

Mode 3 — Soft

The same sine fold, but blended over a soft-clip baseline with no hard clip corner anywhere — and designed so that EQ’ing the folded overshoot (the Delta EQ) never stamps an audible edge onto the waveform. The smoothest, most transparent mode; ideal on the mix bus.

All three modes crossfade seamlessly when switched, so you can A/B them in real time.

6. The three EQs

Three independent 8-band parametric EQs sit at different points in the chain:

  • Input — shapes the signal before it hits the threshold. Use it to pre-emphasise what you want the fold to chew on.
  • Delta — the signature move: this EQ shapes only the folded overshoot, not the in-range signal. Brighten just the detail, or high-pass the fold so the low end clips cleanly while the top folds.
  • Output — conventional post-EQ after the saturation stage, before make-up gain.

Each band offers Bell, Low/High Shelf, Low/High-pass (6–48 dB/oct), Notch, and Band-pass types, 1 Hz–96 kHz frequency, ±24 dB gain, and Q from 0.05 to 40. Each strip has its own bypass.

Working the graph

  • Drag a band handle to set frequency (horizontal) and gain (vertical).
  • The numeric fields below accept typed values — the frequency field understands 2200, 2.2k, or 2.2 kHz, and the Note field accepts pitches like G#5 (A4 = 440 Hz).
  • Drag up/down on a field’s caption to scrub its value (Shift = slower); double-click to reset; scroll to step.
  • The dB scale gutter on the left of the graph is a zoom control — drag or scroll it to change the visible dB range.

7. The saturation section

The fourth tab holds the saturation pad: an XY surface whose corners are four saturator flavours. The signal leaving the fold stage always passes through this blend. Drag the node anywhere on the pad — its position sets the mix of the four corners, and each corner’s live share is shown as a percentage.

CornerCharacter
LinearClean pass-through — the “dry” leg of the blend. The default position is 100% Linear.
JFETSquare-law transistor asymmetry: second-harmonic warmth that thickens as you drive it.
Op-AmpAmplifier-stage rounding. In its normal mode the low end (below ~80 Hz) passes clean while the highs saturate; click its label to switch to Retro, which saturates the full band.
TransformerMagnetic-core character: hysteresis “memory,” even-harmonic bias, and a gentle high-frequency roll-off.

Corner controls

  • Click a corner label to cycle its state: JFET and Transformer step through normal → asymmetry-flip (inverts the harmonic bias) → bypassed; Op-Amp steps On → Retro → Bypass; Linear toggles on/off.
  • A bypassed corner’s share is redistributed to its neighbours — the on-screen percentages always match what you hear.
  • Double-click the pad to reset the whole section (node to Linear, all corners active).
  • The entire section can be bypassed from the Saturation tab’s right-click menu.

All four corners are loudness-matched, so morphing between flavours doesn’t jump in level, and every state change is smoothed — no clicks.

8. Spectrum & oscilloscope

Spectrum analyser

Overlays two curves — the dry signal (after the Input EQ) and the processed signal (after the final stage) — so you can see exactly what the fold and saturation added or tamed.

Oscilloscope

  • Drag horizontally to change the time window (5–500 ms); scroll to zoom.
  • Auto level keeps the trace peak-normalised. Turn it off to set the vertical zoom yourself by dragging up/down.

Both panels are display-only and can be hidden with the Scopes button to shrink the window.

9. Signal flow

From input to output, the chain runs:

  1. Input Gaindrive into the threshold · input meter + VU reference tap right after this stage
  2. Input EQ8-band pre-shaping · spectrum “dry” tap after this stage
  3. Fold-Back Clipperclip + fold per the fold mode; the Delta EQ & Delta level shape the folded overshoot before it’s blended back
  4. Saturation BlendXY mix of Linear / JFET / Op-Amp / Transformer
  5. Output EQ8-band post-shaping
  6. Output Gainmake-up level · output meter + VU tap right after this stage
  7. Brick-Wall Clipfinal bound at 0 dBFS · spectrum “processed” and oscilloscope taps here

All meter and analyser taps are listen-only — they never feed back into the audio path. The Threshold knob’s VU readout is the level ratio between the output-gain tap and the input-gain tap, so it reports the net gain of everything between them (it excludes the input trim itself and the final brick-wall). With oversampling engaged, the entire chain above runs at the elevated rate. There is no global dry/wet control — blending happens inside the Delta stage and on the saturation pad, where the Linear corner acts as the clean leg.

11. Tips

  • Start with Threshold, finish with Delta. Set the amount of clipping first, then dial the folded detail to taste — Delta is the character control.
  • High-pass the Delta EQ around 200–300 Hz to keep the low end clipping cleanly while the mids and highs fold. This is the fastest route to “loud but not mushy.”
  • Mode 3 for buses. When Grandma’s Spice is the last stage on a mix, Mode 3 with a modest Delta keeps things transparent while still catching peaks.
  • Watch the Threshold disc. Its VU-style readout shows the net gain of the processing (from after the input trim to after make-up gain) — aim near 0 dB for honest A/B comparisons.
  • Oversample bright material. Folding generates high harmonics by design; 4× keeps them from aliasing back down on cymbals and distorted synths.
